A note from the author

Most of you know I've spent the last few years living inside the AI machine. Teaching it. Writing about it. Building with it. Breaking it. Trying to make sense of it. From the first ChatGPT moment to today's frontier models and Claude Code, I've watched AI shift shape: tool, advisor, assistant, digital colleague, something that now does real work alongside us. Show full note ↓

The note continues

In one sense the change has been gradual. In another, it's been absurdly fast.

And to say it raises ethical, social, economic, educational and deeply human questions is probably the understatement of our era.

So I kept circling back to a handful of questions I couldn't put down.

Will my children have jobs?

What happens to people who can't use this technology?

Who is accountable when an AI system causes harm?

What happens to education when every student has a tireless digital tutor?

Can we build AI in a way that strengthens human dignity instead of quietly replacing it?

These aren't questions you answer with a product demo, a policy memo, or a LinkedIn hot take.

They need conversation.

So I started experimenting.

At first I built personas to help me think: experts, workers, parents, students, sceptics, optimists, technologists, citizens. I wasn't chasing what to think about AI. I wanted to understand how to think about it.

Then something I didn't plan for happened.

The personas became voices. The voices became characters. The characters eventually became a novel.

I'm calling it TOWNHALL.

The name matters to me. This is where these conversations belong, out in the open. Not only in boardrooms, labs, conferences, classrooms or government committees, but in the civic space where ordinary people get to ask hard questions honestly.

The book is written as a public conversation about AI and the future of work, education, family, responsibility, trust, and the society we're handing to our kids.

It's not meant to settle the debate. It's meant to start one.

My hope is that it helps readers sit with these questions, see them from angles they hadn't considered, and maybe imagine better ways forward, for themselves and for the rest of us.

The first seats in the Town Hall are open.

A few are left.

Take one.

— Victor del Rosal

8:30 PM · About the Book

A single town hall. One evening.
Twelve voices.

The shelf has doom books, hype books, explainer books. It does not have an encounter book, one that refuses both false comfort and false despair.

A single town hall, on one evening in April 2026. Hundreds push back, sometimes win. A frontier-class AI runs live on the moderator's table, projected behind the panel. The system is on examination, not the model.

Eighty-five thousand words. Five to six hours. The follow-up to Humanlike.

10:30 PM · Excerpt

From Chapter I.

Chapter I Opening ≈ 110 words

The hall was already three-quarters full when she walked in. She was carrying a mug, not a notebook. Someone had set the cards on every chair before the doors opened, a single line in serif on cream stock, and as she came down the aisle she could see, here and there, a hand turning one of the cards over to check if there was anything on the back.

There wasn't. Just the four words, and the room. She stepped onto the stage, set the mug on the table beside the laptop, and looked out. The screen behind her was already glowing soft indigo. The model was awake. She was not the only one in the building who had not slept well.

12:30 AM · How this book was made

This is a work of nonfiction commentary in fiction-adjacent form. The town hall is a literary device. The panel and audience characters are composite, drawn from many sources, with plausible bios. No character represents any single real person. The reading list at the back of the book is a curated list of intellectual influences, not a key to the cast.

The model outputs in the dock sessions are real outputs from current frontier-class systems, captured in real sessions on dated days, redacted only to protect personal information. Specific commercial AI products are not named in those scenes. The book is in service of humanity, not a brand.

The book was conceived, directed, and edited by Victor del Rosal, who chose the voices, set the questions, shaped the structure, and made the final editorial judgements throughout. The prose was drafted in dialogue with Claudus, the working name of an AI collaboration Victor has been developing for the past year. Claudus at the time of writing runs on Anthropic's Claude (Opus 4.7), with persistent project memory, a watch list of recurring tics, and a standing simulated reading panel of seven literary intelligences who reviewed the manuscript across three passes. Human authorship is asserted over the work as a whole; AI assistance in the drafting is disclosed here.
